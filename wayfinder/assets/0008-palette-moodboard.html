Gem Miner · Ticket 0008 · Art direction

Two ways the mine could look

The same scene — five strata, six gems, a hardness halo ringing a vein, a lava pocket, and the prize gem glinting through the dark — rendered in each candidate palette at true 16 px. Pick the one whose feel you want; the reserve-saturation rule below is what makes either one legible.

The rule Rock stays desaturated; gems, lava and the glint own the saturated hues. That's why a few bright pixels still read as "not-rock" at the edge of a shrinking view radius.

Resurrect-64

Recommended

Softer, naturalistic, more ramps. 64 colours give clean 3-value rock ramps per band with headroom to spare. Reads as a believable earthy cave — gems glow rather than scream. Costs you nothing extra; the trade is a bigger palette to stay disciplined within.

Endesga-32

tighter · punchier

Higher-contrast, arcade, fewer decisions. 32 colours push more saturation into everything — gems pop harder, but five band ramps + six gems + hazards have to share a tight box, so bands sit closer together in value. Cleaner to manage, less naturalistic.

What to look for in each scene

  • The halo telegraph — the darker, tighter-grained ring of rock hugging each vein. You should see the rock harden a beat before you break into the gem.
  • Lava glows through the dark — even where the darkness overlay is heaviest near the bottom, the hot pocket stays bright. Darkness can't hide a hazard that emits its own light.
  • The prize gem glints at the edge of vision — bottom-right, deepest and darkest band. Its gold cross-glint pierces the overlay while ordinary gems dim. That single readable sparkle is the glimpsed-prize hook.
  • Gems survive the darkness — because they carry the only saturated pixels, tier colours stay distinguishable as the view radius shrinks. That's the reserve-saturation rule paying off.
